> This page is informal and personal. I share my honest impressions of courses I took as an undergraduate at UBC (2020–2024), along with resources I wish I had known earlier. I hope it is useful for students in FRE, Economics, and Land and Food Systems — especially those in their first two years.

---

## How I Got Here

I started UBC in 2020 in the **Land One Program** — a first-year cohort program for students from Forestry and LFS, where you take five courses together and receive unusually strong support. It was, genuinely, one of the best decisions I made. If you are considering LFS, I strongly recommend applying. If you cannot get in, **CONS 200** (taught by Dr. Fernanda Tomaselli) covers similar ground.

After Land One, I majored in **Food and Resource Economics (FRE)**, a small program inspired by the MFRE. Because of its size, the faculty attention is remarkable — my TA positions, reference letters, and several of my most important academic relationships all came from FRE professors.

## Year 1 — Land One (2020/21)

**BIOL 121 — Genetics, Evolution and Ecology** *(Dr. Athena McKown)*
Not hard, and has a lot in common with AP Biology. Dr. Athena is genuinely caring and stays in touch with students long after the course ends.

**LFS 110 — First-Year Integrative Seminar** *(Dr. Fernanda Tomaselli)*
One of the most supportive courses I took. We explored food security, conservation, and global sustainability. If you do not get into Land One, take **CONS 200** — it covers similar ground and is taught by Dr. Fernanda.

**LFS 101 — Principles of Microeconomics** *(Dr. Karen Taylor)*
Most introductory economics courses are taught poorly. This one is not. Dr. Karen is exceptional. The course is restricted to Land One students, but if you end up in a standard ECON 101 section and find it frustrating, know that good teaching of this material exists — and look for supplementary resources like **MRU Microeconomics** (free online).

**PHIL 120 — Introduction to Critical Thinking** *(Dr. Alexandre Korolev)*
Underrated and genuinely useful. The logic you build here pays off when you start proving things in economics — contradiction proofs, welfare theorems, and so on. I also noticed real overlap with the math used in UBC's graduate economics courses. Recommended for anyone considering a further degree in economics.

**LFS 150 — Scholarly Writing and Argumentation** *(Lindsay Cuff)*
Equivalent to WRDS 150. Do not skip it or treat it as a box to check. Learning to write clearly and cite properly early on saves you enormous pain later. The peer-review components are uncomfortable but genuinely valuable.

## Year 2 — FRE Entry (2021/22)

**ECON 221 — Introduction to Strategic Thinking** *(Dr. Hao Li)*
A rigorous game theory course that challenges both logic and intuition. It is 200-level, so it will not count toward an Economics major's 300-level requirements, but the knowledge is worth having — especially if you plan to take **ECON 441** later or pursue graduate study. Dr. Jonathan Graves also teaches this course and is very approachable.

**FRE 306 — Introduction to Global Food Markets** *(Dr. Karen Taylor)*
A good foundation in international trade at the 300-level. The Excess-Supply / Excess-Demand model for trade takes some getting used to, but Dr. Karen teaches it clearly. A natural precursor to ECON 355 or 455.

**ECON 234 — Wealth and Poverty of Nations** *(Dr. Thorsten Rogall)*
One of the best courses I took at UBC. Development economics — institutions, culture, geography, inequality — taught with real intellectual depth. Dr. Thorsten introduced me to instrumental variables and to the idea that correlation and causality are fundamentally different things. That distinction shaped everything I did afterward.

**ECON 255 — Understanding Globalization** *(Dr. Jamie McCasland)*
A paper-reading course focused on globalization, with tutorials and an in-class climate simulation (a WTO role-play). Good preparation for ECON 355 or 455 in terms of intuition.

**ECON 301 — Intermediate Microeconomic Analysis I** *(Dr. Il Myoung Hwang)*
Required for FRE, Economics, and both the MFRE and M.A. Economics programs. It is essentially ECON 101 with calculus. If your math is rusty going in, sort that out first — once the calculus clicks, the economics is manageable.

**PSYC 102 — Intro to Developmental, Social, Personality & Clinical Psychology** *(Dr. Simon Lolliot)*
The best elective I took. Take it for genuine curiosity rather than GPA strategy. The content is fascinating and broadening, even if the reading load is heavier than you expect.

## Year 3 — FRE (2022/23)

**ECON 355 — Introduction to International Trade** *(Dr. Victor Couture)*
A solid introduction to the canonical trade models, with strong real-world examples. Note: credit is only granted for one of ECON 355 or 455. If you want more mathematical depth, 455 is the better choice; if you only need the intuition, 355 is fine.

**FRE 326 — Empirical Methods for Food and Resource Economics** *(Dr. Michael Johnson)*
Genuinely one of the hardest and most useful courses in the program. Covers similar ground to ECON 326 but more hands-on — R, Tableau, group projects, presentations. To get the most out of it, commit fully. One resource I wish I had known: **[COMET](https://comet.arts.ubc.ca/)**, a UBC-built open resource for econometrics in R.

**FRE 385 — Quantitative Methods for Business and Resource Management** *(Dr. Michael Johnson)*
A MFRE course open to undergraduates. Heavy on Excel and practical optimization — shadow prices, sensitivity analysis, linear programming. Applicable in both industry and graduate study. Dr. Michael is the best statistics professor I encountered at UBC; I recommend every course he teaches.

**ECON/FRE 374 — Land and Resource Economics** *(Dr. Frederik Noack)*
A wide-ranging environmental economics course. The lectures reward participation — once you get into Dr. Frederik's rhythm, the material becomes genuinely interesting. Office hours are especially worthwhile here.

**ECON 356 — Introduction to International Finance** *(Dr. Viktoriya Hnatkovska)*
More model-heavy than 355, but Dr. Viktoriya explains the economic intuition clearly enough that you will not get lost if you attend consistently. Traditional format: lectures, assignments, exams, individual study.

## Year 4 — FRE (2023/24)

**ECON 398 — Introduction to Applied Economics** *(Dr. Jonathan Graves)*
The most useful course I took in my undergraduate degree. Causal inference tools — IV, DiD, RDD — taught rigorously and applied to real questions. Even if you are not planning a research career, this course teaches you how to read empirical economics papers critically. It prepares you well for **ECON 425** and **ECON 490**. Do not skip it.

**FRE 474 — Causal Inference in the Economics of Natural Resource Conservation** *(Dr. Sumeet Gulati)*
Complementary to ECON 398, not a substitute. Where 398 gives you balanced theory and hands-on practice, 474 focuses more on paper reading, logic, and replication. You will replicate a published paper and write a referee report — both valuable skills.

**ECON 302 — Intermediate Macroeconomic Analysis I** *(Dr. Emrul Hasan)*
Required for Economics and M.A. Economics. Dr. Emrul makes macroeconomics engaging — attend class and participate, and the content becomes much less dry than it sounds.

**COMR 457 — Fundamentals of Financial Accounting** *(Akash Rattan)*
Surprisingly excellent. The instructor has 20 years of industry experience and it shows. Accounting is one of those skills that almost everyone in economics eventually wishes they had — better to get it early.

**COMR 473 — Business Finance** *(Caren Lombard)*
Fast-paced but not hard if you keep up with the practice problems. Covers valuation, capital structure, and basic corporate finance. Useful for anyone curious about finance applications of economics.

**ECON 303 — Intermediate Microeconomic Analysis II** *(Dr. Il Myoung Hwang)*
Take this with the same instructor as ECON 301 if possible, for continuity. Together they cover the core of intermediate microeconomics.

**FRE 490 — Current Issues in Food and Resource Economics** *(Sean Holowaychuk)*
A project analysis and report-writing course. You will evaluate real-world development projects, identify their failures, and propose improvements. Good training for anyone interested in policy or development work.

**FRST 443 — Remote Sensing for Ecosystem Management** *(Dr. Nicholas Coops)*
An unexpected recommendation for economics students. Remote sensing data is increasingly used in development, environmental, and regional economics research. The 8AM start is painful; the course is worth it.

## General Advice

**On course selection:** Take at least one course outside your comfort zone each year. Some of my most useful knowledge came from PHIL 120, PSYC 102, and FRST 443 — none of which are obvious choices for an economics student.

**On office hours:** Go. Consistently. Every professor I built a meaningful relationship with — and who later supported my applications or gave me research opportunities — I met through office hours, not lectures.

**On math preparation:** If you are considering graduate study in economics, start building your math foundation early. Useful benchmarks are UBC MATH 200 (multivariable calculus) and MATH 221 (linear algebra). Free resources:
- [3Blue1Brown — Essence of Linear Algebra](https://www.3blue1brown.com/topics/linear-algebra) — best for intuition
- [MIT OCW — Multivariable Calculus](https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/18-02-multivariable-calculus-fall-2007/)
- [Axler — Linear Algebra Done Right](https://linear.axler.net/) — free online

**On econometrics:** Start with the intuition before the theory. Two books I recommend to anyone going into economics research:
- *Mostly Harmless Econometrics* — Angrist & Pischke
- *Causal Inference: The Mixtape* — Scott Cunningham ([free online](https://mixtape.scunning.com/))
