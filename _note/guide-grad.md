> This page is informal and personal. I share my experience in the M.A. Economics program at the Vancouver School of Economics (VSE), UBC (2024–2025), along with resources and advice I found genuinely useful. I hope it helps prospective and incoming students navigate the program.

---

## The Program

The **M.A. Economics** at VSE is a one-year, course-based program. It is structured around an intensive first term of four required courses, a more flexible second term of electives, and a summer paper. It is widely considered one of the strongest economics master's programs in Canada.

The program attracts students with varied backgrounds — some heading to industry, some to PhD programs, some to policy. The culture is collaborative, especially in the first term when everyone is in the same courses.

## August–September — Math Camp

A one-month online Math Camp covers the mathematical foundations needed for the program:

- **Basic Python** — operations, vectors and matrices, data frames, loops, linear algebra
- **Linear Algebra** — systems of equations, invertible matrix theorem, least squares, econometric applications
- **Introduction to Proof** — proof operators, structure, and methods
- **Calculus** — derivatives, partial derivatives, integrals, L'Hôpital's rule
- **Optimization** — Lagrangians, Taylor's theorem, Implicit Function Theorem
- **Basic Micro & Macro** — Marshallian and Hicksian demand, Envelope Theorem, DSGE

There are no compulsory assignments or exams, though practice problems are provided. Attendance is not just about the math — it is one of the few chances to get to know your cohort before the intensity of Term 1 begins.

**If your math background is not strong**, I recommend working through the following before the program starts. They are roughly equivalent to what the camp covers:

Linear Algebra (~UBC MATH 221):
- [3Blue1Brown — Essence of Linear Algebra](https://www.3blue1brown.com/topics/linear-algebra) — best for building intuition
- [Axler — Linear Algebra Done Right](https://linear.axler.net/) — free, rigorous textbook
- [The Art of Linear Algebra](https://github.com/kenjihiranabe/The-Art-of-Linear-Algebra) — visual and widely shared

Multivariable Calculus (~UBC MATH 200):
- [MIT OCW — Multivariable Calculus](https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/18-02-multivariable-calculus-fall-2007/) — Dr. Denis Auroux is an excellent lecturer

Computation and Optimization (~UBC ECON 420):
- [Judd — Numerical Methods in Economics](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-3-642-13748-8) — readable and useful as a reference

For econometrics, two faculty pages with excellent reading recommendations:
- [Dr. Jesse Perla — UBC Course Advice](https://www.jesseperla.com/post/ubc-course-advice/)
- [Dr. Jonathan Graves — Advice on Mathematics](https://jonathanlgraves.arts.ubc.ca/advice-on-mathematics/)

## Term 1 (September–December) — Four Required Courses

**ECON 500 — Microeconomics** *(Dr. Vitor Farinha Luz)*

A comprehensive introduction to graduate micro: consumer and firm theory, partial equilibrium, game theory. The material is not entirely new if you have taken intermediate microeconomics, but the mathematical demands are significantly higher. There are roughly eight problem sets over the term — challenging ones.

Two practical notes: discuss the problems with classmates (your submitted work should still be your own), and do not start assignments before you have understood the relevant lecture material. Rushing in with incomplete understanding costs more time than it saves.

The main textbook used in most graduate programs is **Mas-Colell, Whinston & Green — *Microeconomic Theory*** (MWG). A more accessible alternative is **Jehle & Reny — *Advanced Microeconomic Theory***. Dr. Vitor's lecture notes are thorough, so the textbook is supplementary rather than required.

**ECON 502 — Macroeconomics** *(Dr. Viktoriya Hnatkovska)*

Graduate macro differs from undergraduate macro in focus and method. Rather than describing the economy, you build micro-founded models of it. Topics include growth models (Malthusian, Solow-Swan, RCK, AK), consumption, employment, asset pricing, general equilibrium, and real business cycles. Optimization is constant — Lagrangians, Hamiltonians, saddle paths, and steady states appear throughout. There is also some coding in Matlab and Dynare.

The textbook is **David Romer — *Advanced Macroeconomics*** (4th ed.). It is slightly dated relative to cutting-edge research, but works well for the course. For something more current: [PhD Macro Book](https://phdmacrobook.org/tableofcontents/) (free online).

**ECON 526 — Quantitative Economics with Data Science Applications** *(Dr. Jesse Perla & Dr. Paul Schrimpf)*

One of the most useful courses in the program. The first half focuses on working with data and the linear algebra behind it; the second half covers empirical causal inference. Course materials are available publicly on GitHub, and the [QuantEcon](https://quantecon.org/) project provides supplementary material.

Key readings used in the course:
- [CausalML Book](https://causalml-book.org/) — more technical
- [Causal Inference for the Brave and True](https://matheusfacure.github.io/python-causality-handbook/) — more readable, free online

**ECON 527 — Econometrics** *(Dr. Kyungchul Song)*

The theoretical counterpart to ECON 526. Covers identification, estimation, and asymptotic inference. Topics include a statistics review, regression as prediction and as causal inference (IV), and the potential outcomes framework (RCT, DiD, propensity score matching). The course textbook is written by Dr. Song and is exceptionally clear.

Auxiliary references: *Econometrics* (Hayashi), *Econometric Analysis* (Greene), *Econometric Theory and Methods* (Davidson & MacKinnon).

## Term 2 (January–April) — Electives

The second term is more flexible. PhD and MA students share classrooms, with different grading rubrics — MA grades typically weight exams more heavily, while PhD grades often include research proposals and presentations.

If you are considering the **thesis stream** (a two-year program where the second year functions as a pre-doctoral research year), Term 2 is the time to approach faculty and have those conversations.

**ECON 544 — Political Economy** *(Dr. Nathan Nunn)*

More accurately described as economic history with cultural and anthropological context. The course asks why some societies are wealthy and others are not, using history, institutions, and culture as the primary lenses. The reading load is substantial — papers assigned weekly. For most papers, reading the abstract, introduction, and conclusion is sufficient to engage with the key arguments.

**ECON 546 — Monetary Theory and Policy** *(Dr. Paul Beaudry)*

Dr. Beaudry served as Deputy Governor of the Bank of Canada, and it shows — the course bridges theoretical monetary models and their real-world application (or failure to apply). Heavy use of ECON 502 tools. Recommended if you enjoyed macro and are interested in how central banks actually operate.

**ECON 523 — Behavioural Economics** *(Dr. Matt Lowe)*

The most enjoyable course I took in the program. Behavioural economics integrates psychology into economic models — examining how people deviate from the rational agent assumption and what that implies for markets and policy. The course ends with a student presentation on an open question or real-world application of your choice.

**ECON 550 — Public Economics** *(Dr. Marit Rehavi)*

A rigorous treatment of market failure and government responses: insurance, moral hazard, adverse selection, and the limits of public intervention. The intellectual core is the tension between individual incentives and social outcomes. Taught seriously and professionally.

## Summer (May–August) — Applied Paper

Near the end of Term 2, students choose between the thesis stream and the original stream. The original stream ends with a summer paper written under faculty supervision.

**ECON 594 — Applied Economics** *(Dr. Sam Norris)*

The course teaches academic paper writing: abstract, introduction, literature review, empirical strategy, and results. The most valuable element is working closely with your advisor on research you actually care about. Treat it as real research, not coursework — the quality of what you produce reflects the seriousness with which you approach it.

Go to office hours. Take the writing feedback seriously. The gap between a competent paper and a good one is almost always in the introduction and the clarity of the research question.

## General Advice

**On the jump from undergraduate to graduate study:** The difficulty increase is real, especially in Term 1. The math is harder, the pace is faster, and the expectation of independent thinking is higher. This is normal — give yourself time to adjust.

**On problem sets:** Collaborate with classmates to understand the problems, but write your own solutions. The process of writing things out in your own words is where the learning actually happens.

**On choosing electives:** Pick courses that align with what you want to write your summer paper on. The elective term and the paper are connected — the paper goes better when it grows out of something you have been thinking about for months.

**On the thesis vs. course stream:** Talk to current students and recent graduates of both streams before deciding. The thesis stream is demanding but provides strong preparation for a PhD or a pre-doctoral fellowship. The course stream is more structured and suits students heading to policy or industry.

**On resources:**
- [QuantEcon](https://quantecon.org/) — computational economics, Python and Julia
- [Causal Inference: The Mixtape](https://mixtape.scunning.com/) — free; best applied intro to the methods used throughout the program
- [The Effect](https://theeffectbook.net/) — free; accessible and example-driven
- [PhD Macro Book](https://phdmacrobook.org/) — free; more current than Romer for macro
