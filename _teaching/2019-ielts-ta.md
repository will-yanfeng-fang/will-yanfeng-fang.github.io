## About IELTS

The International English Language Testing System (IELTS) is one of the two main English proficiency tests for international students looking to study in English-speaking countries — the other being TOEFL. Newer alternatives like Duolingo have also emerged, though I am less familiar with those.

IELTS has four sections: **Listening**, **Reading**, and **Writing** are taken on the same day, while the **Speaking** test is scheduled independently (though it can be done on the same day with some flexibility).

> **2026 update:** As of March 2026, IELTS has officially announced that the paper-based test will be discontinued globally from mid-2026. All tests will be delivered on computer. In selected markets, a "Writing on Paper" hybrid option will be available — allowing candidates to still handwrite their Writing responses while completing Listening and Reading on screen. The test content, scoring, and band scale remain unchanged. See the [official announcement](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/updates-to-ielts-test-delivery) for details.

## Listening — best score: 8

There are four sections of increasing difficulty.

- **Section 1** covers everyday life situations; you fill in blanks based on a conversation.
- **Section 2** sometimes involves a graphic element — for example, following directions on a map and labelling street names.
- **Sections 3 & 4** are academic conversations, resembling real university lectures.

Most students find this section accessible. Practice makes perfect!

---

## Reading — best score: 7

You have one hour and three passages to work through. Full comprehension is not strictly necessary — look for keywords and synonyms that hint at where answers appear. A useful trick: often only the first, second, and last sentence of each paragraph are needed.

---

## Writing — best score: 6.5

The hardest section for most students. Many get stuck at 5.5 despite multiple attempts, while most universities require at least 6 in every section. You have one hour split across two tasks:

**Task 1 — "small paper" (15–20 minutes)**

Describe a chart, graph, pie chart, or boxplot. Accuracy and clarity are key.

**Task 2 — "big paper" (40–45 minutes)**

Present and argue a position on a given topic (e.g., *Should we use nuclear power?*). Logic is what separates a 5.5 from a 6 — make sure you are answering the question, structuring your ideas clearly, and emphasising the right points.

**Recommended resource:** 《慎小嶷十天突破雅思写作》 — a Chinese-language book containing real past questions and sample essays graded from 5.5 to 9. I recommend reading the 8 and 8.5 band papers rather than the 9s, as the 9-band essays are written by examiners and are not the most useful models for test-takers. The author also wrote a speaking book, though it is less compelling.

## Speaking — best score: 7

Practice, practice, practice. There are no real tricks here — as long as you speak logically, answer the question, and keep talking fluently, you will be fine. My first TA role involved running mock speaking tests and having English conversations with students to keep them immersed in the language.

There are three parts:

**Part 1 — casual chat**

Basic questions about yourself: name, hometown, favourite colour, etc. This part determines whether you score above or below 5.

**Part 2 — prepared monologue**

The examiner gives you a prompt (e.g., *Describe your favourite song — its genre, what it is about, who enjoys it, and why you like it*). You have 1 minute to prepare with a scratch paper, then speak for up to 2 minutes. Aim for at least 1 minute 40 seconds; the examiner will stop you if you exceed 2 minutes. This part roughly determines whether you reach 6.5.

**Part 3 — open discussion**

A follow-up conversation on broader themes, without any preparation time. Confidence matters as much as language ability here — practice in real, unscripted English conversations is the best preparation.
