## About AP & IELTS

My high school, Shenzhen (Nanshan) Concord College of Sino-Canada, provided significant support for students preparing for AP, IELTS, and TOEFL. But I believe that no matter which school you attend, hard work (and a bit of luck) goes a long way. As a traditional Chinese — and assuming my audience here is likely of a similar background — studying remains one of the safest and highest-return investments you can make as a young person, at least before university. Below is my advice on AP and IELTS. For employers who want to see my AP or IELTS transcripts, please send me a request.

> **2026 update — AP goes digital:** College Board has been transitioning AP exams to its Bluebook digital testing app. As of 2025–2026, most AP exams are either fully digital (both multiple-choice and free-response answered in Bluebook) or hybrid digital (multiple-choice in Bluebook, free-response handwritten in a paper booklet). The exams I took that are now **hybrid** in Bluebook include: Calculus AB & BC, Micro & Macroeconomics, Statistics, Chemistry, Biology, and Physics 1. **Environmental Science** is now **fully digital**. Content, scoring, and difficulty are unchanged — but students should practise in the Bluebook app before exam day. See the [College Board's official overview](https://apstudents.collegeboard.org/ap-exams-what-to-know/digital-testing-exam-modes) for the latest by subject.

## Advanced Placement (AP)

The AP programme is designed to prepare students for first-year university coursework. It can also earn transfer credit (typically with a score of ≥3), giving you flexibility in how you approach your degree. There are three broad paths:

1. **Take the AP → take the same university course anyway** — as a GPA booster or to solidify your knowledge.
2. **Take the AP → skip the equivalent university course → enjoy a lighter course load** — more time for extracurriculars and a diverse university experience.
3. **Take the AP → skip the equivalent university course → graduate early.**

I took path 2. But be aware that some level of self-study and extra commitment is required. For example, if you take AP Calculus AB & BC and AP Micro & Macroeconomics, you can potentially skip MATH 104 & 105 and ECON 101 & 102 at UBC — meaning you could enter ECON 301 & 302 (Intermediate Micro & Macroeconomics) in your second year. The challenge: how do you keep up with 300-level courses if you haven't touched the fundamentals for a year? AP is still technically high school level, and the gap can feel steep.

Furthermore, if you are aiming for a master's or PhD, you will eventually need more mathematically rigorous economics courses, plus linear algebra, econometrics, real analysis, and proof-based math — all of which build on strong first-year foundations. If you only have AP credit, prepare for an adjustment.

**My recommendation:** choose path 1 if you are confident about pursuing graduate school; path 3 if you have strong constraints and need to enter the workforce quickly; path 2 if you want a well-rounded, flexible university life.

## AP courses I have taken

### AP Calculus AB — score: 5

*(Hybrid digital in Bluebook from 2025)*

Covers differential calculus. If you have taken Pre-Calculus and are comfortable with a graphing calculator, this is very manageable. No real tricks — practice makes perfect. Note that some universities, like U of T, may not grant transfer credit for AB alone; you may need both AB and BC. At UBC, AB and BC can be used separately (AB = MATH 104, BC = MATH 105). Transfer credit policies change, so consult your university advisor for the latest.

### AP Calculus BC — score: 5

*(Hybrid digital in Bluebook from 2025)*

Complementary to AB, covering integral calculus. I encourage students to aim for at least a 4, ideally a 5 — it is very achievable with consistent effort. One quirk when I took it (2019): if you took both AB and BC, the grade displayed for both was the average of the two scores. Khan Academy and various YouTube channels are excellent free resources, and some are honestly better than paid instruction — though self-study requires strong intrinsic motivation. Math is the most important foundational subject for most university majors; do not skip it.

### AP Microeconomics — score: 5

*(Hybrid digital in Bluebook from 2025)*

A no-brainer for most students — easy, useful, and it builds the economic intuition that helps you understand how the world works. No math beyond counting and multiplying, though knowing calculus helps with concepts like marginal revenue. Knowing how to draw supply-and-demand graphs is essential. Transfers as ECON 101 at UBC and equivalents. Khan Academy is excellent for this course.

### AP Macroeconomics — score: 5

*(Hybrid digital in Bluebook from 2025)*

Similar in style to Micro but slightly harder — I regularly see students struggle here. I recommend taking Micro first. Covers policy, international finance, and government intervention. Transfers as ECON 102 at UBC and equivalents. Khan Academy covers this well too.

### AP Statistics — score: 4

*(Hybrid digital in Bluebook from 2025)*

If you are unsure about your major, statistics is never a wrong choice. The course has two halves: descriptive statistics (straightforward) and inferential statistics (a bit tricky, and requires some graphing calculator skill). If you are heading toward economics, statistics is critical — econometrics and causal inference are built on it. Good economic intuition without statistical tools will only take you so far.

### AP Physics 1 — score: 4

*(Hybrid digital in Bluebook from 2025)*

More of an eye-opener than a deep dive. Requires solid understanding of formulas, physical intuition, and some memorisation. Less useful for most science pathways than Physics C, according to science friends of mine — but a solid introduction nonetheless.

### AP Environmental Science — score: 4

*(Fully digital in Bluebook from 2025)*

Another good eye-opener course. This one is very self-study-friendly — I prepared with a few friends and occasional support from a teacher (thank you, Teacher Lina Sun!).

### AP Chemistry — score: 4

*(Hybrid digital in Bluebook from 2025)*

Harder than Physics 1 and Environmental Science. Requires logical thinking and some genuine interest in chemistry. This was the first course that taught me how to approach problems using both quantitative and qualitative methods. Equivalent to UBC CHEM 121 — students I have TA'd at UBC tell me that course is brutal, so the AP route might be worth considering if you want to get ahead.

### AP Biology — score: 3

*(Hybrid digital in Bluebook from 2025)*

The hardest AP I took, and my lowest score. The textbook is as thick as a university text, and I will be honest — I did not take it seriously enough. I also just do not love biology. That said, it carries significant transfer credit value precisely because universities recognise its difficulty.
