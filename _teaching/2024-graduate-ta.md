## ECON 221 — Introduction to Strategic Thinking

**What this course is about:** This course introduces the core concepts of non-cooperative game theory — sequential games, simultaneous-move games, mixed strategies, repeated games, and mechanism design. The goal is to help students recognize strategic structure in everyday social interactions, not just in abstract models.

**Textbook:** *Games of Strategy* (6th ed.) by Dixit, Skeath & McAdams — available through the UBC Bookstore in print or online. The book is clear and example-driven, which makes it unusually readable for a theory course.

**TA's advice:**
- Game theory feels intuitive at first but gets tricky once mixed strategies and repeated games arrive. Don't fall behind — each lecture builds on the last.
- The homework assignments are graded on *effort*, not correctness, so use them as genuine practice. Write out your reasoning step-by-step even if you are unsure of the answer.
- For the open-book midterm and final, a handwritten summary of key solution concepts (Nash equilibrium, backward induction, subgame perfection) is more useful than trying to bring everything. Knowing *when* to apply a concept matters more than memorizing definitions.
- Office hours are the best place to work through specific game trees or payoff matrices you find confusing — bring a concrete example.

**Useful free resources:**
- [Yale ECON 159 — Game Theory (Ben Polak)](https://oyc.yale.edu/economics/econ-159) — full lecture videos; excellent companion to the textbook
- [Crash Course on Nash Equilibrium (Econimate)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M3oWYHYoBvk)
- [Game Theory 101 (William Spaniel)](https://gametheory101.com/) — short, focused video explanations of individual concepts

## ECON 226 — Making Sense of Economic Data

**What this course is about:** An applied econometrics course that develops students' ability to read and critically evaluate empirical economic research. Topics include regression analysis, causal inference, difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity, and hypothesis testing. Readings draw from published papers in development economics, labor economics, and public policy.

**TA's advice:**
- The hardest part of this course is not the math — it is learning to *interpret* coefficients carefully, with attention to units, sign, and statistical significance all at once. Practice writing out full, precise interpretations of every coefficient you encounter.
- Understand what a p-value *actually* means (the probability of observing a result at least as extreme as the one found, *if the null hypothesis were true*) — students who confuse this with "the probability the null is true" run into trouble on exams.
- Omitted variable bias (OVB) is a recurring theme. For any observational regression, always ask: what unobserved variable might be correlated with both the treatment and the outcome?
- When reading papers (Glewwe et al., Dube et al., Card 1990, etc.), focus on the research design first — what is the identification strategy and why does it help establish causality?

**Key concepts to master early:**
- Confidence intervals and what "95% CI" means practically
- The difference between descriptive and causal inference
- What "parallel trends" requires in a diff-in-diff design

**Useful free resources:**
- [Causal Inference: The Mixtape (Scott Cunningham)](https://mixtape.scunning.com/) — free online; excellent applied treatment of the methods used in this course
- [Nick Huntington-Klein's *The Effect*](https://theeffectbook.net/) — another free, accessible book on causal inference with real-world examples
- [Khan Academy: Statistics & Probability](https://www.khanacademy.org/math/statistics-probability) — good review of confidence intervals and hypothesis testing fundamentals

## LFS 252 — Quantitative Data Analysis

**What this course is about:** An introduction to statistical reasoning and data analysis for students in Land and Food Systems. Topics typically include descriptive statistics, probability, sampling distributions, hypothesis testing, and regression — applied to environmental and agricultural data.

**TA's advice:**
- Many students arrive with limited statistics background and feel intimidated early on. The key is consistent practice: work through problems by hand before relying on software, so you understand what the calculations actually represent.
- When interpreting results, always connect the number back to the real-world context. A p-value below 0.05 is not the end of the story — ask whether the effect size is practically meaningful.
- Data cleaning and visualization are skills that pay off far beyond this course. Spend time on them even when they are not heavily graded.

**Useful free resources:**
- [StatQuest with Josh Starmer (YouTube)](https://www.youtube.com/c/joshstarmer) — outstanding visual explanations of statistics fundamentals and machine learning concepts
- [R for Data Science (Hadley Wickham)](https://r4ds.had.co.nz/) — free online; ideal if R is used in the course
- [OpenIntro Statistics](https://www.openintro.org/book/os/) — free, well-written introductory textbook

## FRST/LFS 101 — Principles of Microeconomics

**What this course is about:** A standard introductory microeconomics course covering supply and demand, elasticity, consumer and producer theory, market structures (perfect competition, monopoly, oligopoly), externalities, and public goods. Readings and examples emphasize the Canadian and global economy.

**Textbook:** *Microeconomics: Canada in the Global Environment* (9th ed.) by Parkin & Bade.

**TA's advice:**
- Economics is learned by *doing*, not just reading. After each chapter, work through end-of-chapter problems — this is the single best way to prepare for exams.
- Supply and demand graphs are the backbone of the course. Invest time early in understanding *why* each curve shifts, not just whether it shifts left or right. Every later topic (externalities, price controls, taxation) is an extension of this framework.
- Active note-taking in lecture matters. Write down the professor's verbal explanations of graphs and the examples they give spontaneously — these regularly appear on exams in a way that slides alone do not capture.
- For multi-choice and short-answer exams: read each question carefully for what it is *not* asking. Many errors come from answering a related but different question.

**Useful free resources:**
- [MRU Microeconomics (Marginal Revolution University)](https://mru.org/principles-economics-microeconomics) — free, high-quality video series aligned closely with a standard intro micro curriculum
- [Khan Academy: Microeconomics](https://www.khanacademy.org/economics-finance-domain/microeconomics) — reliable for reviewing individual topics
- [Economics Explained (YouTube)](https://www.youtube.com/c/EconomicsExplained) — accessible real-world context for core concepts
